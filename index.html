Stones Stand Witness in Silent Response
Stefaniia Bodnia
2024

Abstract

Micro-particles, dust, fossils, metals, gases, and minerals compose an archive of the memory of life and death within the terrain. It holds the memory of loss and destruction, engaging in the interplay between political dynamics, material exchange, and the production of technologies. Matter serves as a starting point for better understanding the materiality of these technologies and exploration of power dynamics that create layers of controlled visibility.

This research explores the landscape of south-eastern Ukraine across three stories of its past, post-industrial present, and service-oriented future, marked by the enduring impact of imperialism. The landscape, disintegrating into minerals, transforms into dust, micro particles resembling black snow, and neon gas—the material and matter holding power and bearing witness.

Introduction

The profound sense of loss is purely striking. Loss as a long and slow gruelling process, there is a timeline in its concept. The interconnected nature of it is present across scales – from personal to global, from human to non-human, from micro to the planetary – all of this makes the loss collective. The loss of the loved one, ecological catastrophe – the bodies reach their limits. Entanglement occurs when these bodies appear intangibly connected to each other and share a sort of same idea. Taking a perspective from quantum theory, one can even perceive the potential coherence between entities on a quantum scale.

In this research I explore notions of matter through historical, ecological and political perspectives. The micro-particles, dust, gas atoms and metals are the forms of matter transforming into materials through the process of passive accumulation. Tim Ingold argues that ‘materials do not exist; in the manner of objects, they rather occur, carry on, or perdure, through time’.[1] Each material, in fact, represents a state of becoming—the potential to transform into something else. In this context, gas atoms, minerals can be seen as a material in the process of being transformed into commodity for the productions of technologies. Deleuze writes on materiality: 'whenever we encounter matter ‘it is matter in movement, in flux, in variation’, with the consequence that ‘this matter-flow can only be followed’.[]

Non-human dimensions are not accessible to the human eye without machine vision. Micro-particles are not directly visible matter. Despite their minuscule size, they wield power in capital and political competition when they are transformed into material. In this essay, I talk about layers of visibility and their intersection with the forces of power that control them. Obscurity is the core element of this essay; I consider it through its different dimensions. What are the invisibilities on micro and planetary scale? When does invisible become visible?

The materiality of technology consists of the processes of creating these technologies based on colonial and imperial legacy: as a continuation of the conquest and accumulation of the land and resources. The material composition of technologies includes minerals, metals, gases and various elements that are integrated into objects. We cannot imagine the future without looking into past. In the first chapter, “Night Blankets the Landscape”, I use a night painting of an 18th century of landscape as a lens to talk about resource extraction, exploitation and imperial othering on the territory of southern-eastern Ukraine. Are there ways to trade materials without the cover of darkness? What is the materiality of labor and what role does it play in the material exchange? How does the relationship between technology's materiality and physically demanding labor of a region's population lead to their exploitation and invisibility, and how does this affect the environment, society, and politics in the long term?

The second chapter, “Black snow”, unfolds the story of how emissions from industrial enterprises on a winter morning in Mariupol, a city in eastern Ukraine, resulted in grey-tinted surfaces. Where micro-particles remain invisible in everyday situations, their presence becomes apparent when they interact with the white snow. Dust particles of the black snow carry political agency as lungs inhale dormant political ambition. Their accumulation over time inside Earth and bodies becomes a witness to the events that take place on this territory.

The third chapter “Neon Futures” delves into the political power of neon as a gas used for the production of semi-conductors. It raises the history of investment into the production of this gas in south-eastern Ukraine, including the metallurgical plant in Mariupol. Neon particles gain their material power as a vibrant matter, revealing the workings of power structures – the control, exploitation and destruction of land in the relentless pursuit of value production.

Chapter I

Night Blankets the Landscape

Arkhip Kuindzhi, Moonlit Night on the Dnepr/Nightonthe Dnepr, 1880. Oil on canvas, 105 × 144 cm. State Russian Museum, St. Petersburg.

The moon, the clouds, and the dark distance. The painting by Mariupol-born artist Arkhip Kuindzhi depicts an innocent landscape–the two shores of Ukraine in 1880, before its industrial transformation. In this essay, landscape is not just a painting, it is a lens, it is a universal medium, denaturalisation representational physicality of nature and its representation through the arts lens. [1] I use the medium of the landscape to talk about its exploitation, depletion, geopolitical and ecological processes that are linked to the materiality of technologies produced within this region.

An image of a landscape can be defined as a particular tracing of the place and history. The image serves as a representation of data through a particular form of analysis. Landscape image is a classical mode of power that emerges through representation. In thus case, the establishment of the notion of the territory or the landscape emerges by way of the technics of landscape painting. Everything depicted in the painting vanishes into the obscurity of an unstable blend of paint and pigments. The artist used aluminum dust to intensify the radiance and luminosity, while bitumen or liquid asphalt, darkening with time, obscures the depicted details [2] Over time, the image transforms gradually dissolving into an abstraction of darkness and invisibility. The process of pigment fading is intertwined with a historical context that voids the events of transformation of the landscape. What remains obscured within the framework of landscape industrial transformation? What persists in invisibility? What constitutes the material essence of an industrial landscape? What is the landscape beyond the industrial transformation?

Art historian Hélène Valence explains how vision and technological progress intersect. She argues that nocturnes, or night paintings, serve as a response to the increased technological visibility of the age. These works consider two dimensions of visibility—the physically observable and that which is within the limits of the day's gaze. Seeking to distance themselves from the visibility imposed by technology, the artists found inspiration in an alternative, introspective form of vision. In their creations, they sought to establish a formal connection with this inward-looking perspective, directing their attention to the darkness opened up by the remarkable discoveries of their time. [3].

The discovery of coal and complex ores deposits from Donbas to the Dnipro river led to the transformation of this landscape. The demand for the minerals from Donbas and neighbouring territory has consistently been a direct result of industrial developments, making this region soil the birthplace of site of extraction and destruction that are actualised in other realities. Mining and industrialisation are an attempt to capitalise on the geologically depleted planetary body. Metallurgical plants, chemical production, and mining of rare materials, including noble gases like neon, krypton, xenon and helium, (see Neon Futures chapter) turned the landscape into the resource material extracted and exploited for capitalistic profit.

The transformation of the landscape involves a process of detachment and abstraction. Earth undergoes a sorting process, breaking down into minerals that eventually turn into refined dust. As the landscape undergoes these changes, it retains evidence of events that occur within it. ‘The black myth’ of Donbas encapsulates the reduction of the region within the cultural imagination to a mere extraction resource and component of material exchange[4]. This process equates the human population and life at large to geological, agricultural, and other forms of matter with usable material capacities. This reduction embodies a form of slow violence and environmental damage inflicted on the region, often characterised through the lens of the inhuman category. 

During the 19th century devDelopment of the field of geology in imperial nations, geological prospecting initiated in the southeast regions of Ukraine. This, in turn, catalysed significant industrialisation processes primarily fuelled by European and Russian investments, coinciding with the advent of "steel fever." By 1900, a "favorable investment climate" spurred the extensive extraction of coal and iron ore, along with the concurrent expansion of metallurgical and chemical industries, mechanical engineering, and the growth of agro-industry in the region[5]. Therefore, Donbas attracted investments from Belgian, Dutch, British, and American industrialists, with a primary emphasis on mining activities that generated substantial profits, particularly benefiting the elite of the Russian Empire. Ukraine found itself subjected to a form of dual colonisation by both Western Europe and the Russian Empire, a condition that was further solidified during the Soviet era.[6]

Classical power is often transmitted through landscape painting, and Kuindzhi's work serves as a vivid illustration of the assertion of ownership of the landscape. The painting, held in the State Russian Museum in St Petersburg, itself signifies the authoritative appropriation of territory and reflects the colonial attitude toward Ukrainian land. This portrayal ties into Jason W. Moore's concept of "peak appropriation," emphasising the historical capitalist trends of enclosure and depletion of natural resources. Moore contends that the key issue faced by capital today is not abstract depletion but the diminishing opportunities to appropriate nature inexpensively. This idea encapsulates "the long history of enclosure and exhaustion of coal seams, oil fields, aquifers, and peasantries across the space and time of historical capitalism. In this light, the chief problem is not 'peak everything' but peak appropriation. Capital's problem today is not depletion in the abstract but the contracting opportunities to appropriate nature cheaply." [7]. The intertwining of the materiality of work and the eternal nature of the Earth's materiality encompasses processes such as photosynthesis, the use of fossil fuels and the contemporary importance of rare earth minerals.[8] These elements serve both as reminders of geological time scales and as essential components mined for the development of technology.

In addition to the Earth’s material, the human labour of the region’s population also participates in the material exchange. Materiality of technologies and immateriality of hard and physically exhausting labour are intertwined. Miners, factory workers, and employees of enterprises embody the tangible aspect, often reduced to mere instruments of production, living material or inhuman subject. For decades, the West Europeans and Russians have rendered Ukrainians as inexpensive labour, the resource — the bodies sustaining the environmental impact of their industries, exhausted by work-related injuries, the bodies whose cancer rates dictated by others’ business interests. These bodies are the buffer zone, the production site, a population whose voice is muted or falsified by political power. They are ‘invisible’ bodies belonging to inhuman category. Bodies in which the dust of industrialisation settles, which are now forced to migrate. They are not present in the landscape, both in the picture and in the deserted, ruined land.  

Now this landscape transformed into post-industrial, empty site reclaiming the space abandoned by people after mining operations have ceased. This space is marked by dust, rocks, mines, destruction, entropy and the will to resist it—the will to pull and grasp something that is continually dissipating: the unknowability of the day we might return and the world will return to the point when there was no ‘before’. The destruction and war invented the longing and distance within us. It is invisible feeling, yet it weighs like a massive rock hewn from beneath the earth. This rock, created from the accumulation of settled dust in the lungs, holds memories akin to the way the Earth captures and remembers events that occur on it.

Conclusion

The interplay between microchip technologies, political dynamics and imperial ambitions prevents us from better understanding the materiality of these technologies and penetrating the power dynamic that creates layers of controlled visibilities. Today we must come to terms with the relative lack of power in the face of obscured systems. This essay surveys the landscape of the south-eastern Ukraine thought the lens of past, post-industrial present and service-oriented future. It is marked by the impact of colonialism on the landscape and its inhabitants thought the lens of a matter as a resource. The landscape breaking down into minerals turned into dust, micro particles of black snow and neon gas – the matter characterising the place thought the material prospective: imperial gase, exploitation, ecological damage, destruction and war.
